# IA'm and C2PA: Complementary Layers of AI Transparency

*A positioning note — May 2026*

---

## Two layers, one ecosystem

The global response to AI-generated content has produced two distinct families of transparency tools.

**Technical provenance** records *what produced the file*. Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) standards, Content Credentials, watermarking, cryptographic signing — all answer the question: *which tool signed this, and from what source was it derived?*

**Semantic declaration** records *who decided what the work would be*. IA'm is the first structured framework in this layer. It answers a different question: *who carries the creative decision?*

These are not competing standards. They are layers in a stack — one cryptographic, one semantic — that can coexist on the same file and address different audiences.

## What C2PA does well

C2PA, governed under the Linux Foundation since 2021 and now backed by an ecosystem of 6,000+ members, is the de facto standard for machine-readable AI provenance. The EU AI Act Code of Practice (second draft, March 2026) explicitly adopts a multi-layered approach in which C2PA metadata embedding is a core component.

C2PA establishes:

- A cryptographically signed manifest binding the file to its origin
- A traceable chain of edits and ingredients (parents, components, inputs)
- AI-generation status as a declared assertion
- Verification through certificate authorities on a trust list

C2PA is technical infrastructure. It is read by machines and surfaced to humans through platform integrations (Adobe Content Credentials icon, Microsoft Edge verification, browser plug-ins).

## What C2PA does not do

C2PA does not qualify the **nature of human involvement**. A file signed as "generated by Firefly" tells the viewer the tool. It does not tell them whether the human:

- wrote a single prompt and accepted the first output,
- iterated through forty refinements with deliberate aesthetic choices,
- or used the tool as a discrete step within a larger human-led process.

These distinctions are central to creative authorship, to copyright doctrine (where human decision-making is the threshold for protection in most jurisdictions), and to public trust in cultural production. C2PA was not designed to capture them, and its technical structure is not the right place for them.

## Where IA'm fits

IA'm operates as a **semantic layer above the technical one**. Its criterion — *who carries the creative decision?* — is qualitative, declared by the creator, and structured in three levels:

| Level | Decision authority |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Assisté** | Human; AI as task helper |
| **Initié** | Shared, human-led |
| **Généré** | Delegated to AI on human instruction |

A work made without AI requires no badge. The absence of an IA'm declaration is itself meaningful.

This declaration is:

- **Voluntary** — opt-in by the creator, not imposed at the platform layer
- **Upstream** — applied at creation, before distribution
- **Human-readable** — visible to any viewer without specialized tools
- **Free to use** — released under Creative Commons BY-NC 4.0
- **Technology-agnostic** — independent of which AI tool, which platform, which file format

## Compatibility, not redundancy

A single image, video, or text can carry both:

- A **C2PA manifest** declaring the cryptographic chain of production
- An **IA'm badge** declaring the level of human creative authority

The two layers answer to different stakeholders. C2PA serves platforms, regulators, forensic verification, and automated detection. IA'm serves the reader, the viewer, the buyer, the audience — those who want to know not only *was this made by AI*, but *what kind of human shaped it*.

## Regulatory positioning

Current and forthcoming frameworks — EU AI Act Article 50 (applicable August 2026), California SB 942, China's CAC labeling measures (in force since September 2025), India's draft IT rules — regulate the **downstream**: platforms, deployers, distribution. They impose machine-readable marking and, in some cases, visible disclosure.

These frameworks govern the moment of publication. They do not govern the moment of creation. IA'm proposes a complementary creator-led declaration that:

- Predates the regulated downstream marking
- Provides qualitative context that downstream marking cannot carry
- Operates in jurisdictions and creative contexts where no obligation yet exists

The French presumption introduced by the *Loi Darcos* (April 2026) — that protected works have presumptively been used by AI training — addresses upstream rights. The EU AI Act addresses downstream transparency. IA'm proposes a tool for the **declarative middle**: what the creator chooses to say about their own process.

## Summary

| | C2PA / Content Credentials | IA'm |
|---|---------------------------|------|
| Layer | Cryptographic provenance | Semantic declaration |
| Question answered | What tool produced this? | Who carried the creative decision? |
| Mode | Machine-readable, signed | Human-readable, declared |
| Application point | At signing / generation | At creator's choice |
| Cost | ~$289/year per certificate | Free (CC BY-NC 4.0) |
| Governance | Linux Foundation, 6000+ members | Independent creator, INPI n° 5212301 |
| Compatible with the other? | Yes | Yes |

The two were not designed against each other. They were designed for different questions. AI transparency will require both.
